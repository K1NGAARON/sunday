What is remote employee onboarding?

Whether it may be in the office or at home, onboarding plays a critical role in a new hire's success and happiness. There are several ways to ensure that new, remote hires are properly supported during their first few days on the job. One of the most effective methods is to use a formal onboarding process. But what does remote employee onboarding entail?

There are many different approaches to onboard your new hires, but the meaning and the goals remain the same whether it’s virtual or at the office.

The definition

(Remote) Onboarding /ˈɒnbɔːdɪŋ,ɒnˈbɔːdɪŋ/

Remote onboarding is the process of incorporating a new employee into a company and familiarizing them with the company culture and policies, so they can become an effective and contributing member of the team implementing technology of video calls and tech tools.

Onboarding is a critical employee engagement process that defines, creates, & promotes culture. An effective onboarding plan will help employees feel comfortable with their new role and company culture. This includes providing training and support as needed. But what are the goals of remote onboarding?

Remote onboarding goals

From an employee's perspective, an onboarding program is an opportunity to get used to a new environment. For an employer, it's a clearly pre-defined, thought-through and goal-oriented process, designed to achieve the following objectives:

  • Help the company leave a good first impression on the new employee;
  • Help the new employee feel welcome and feel like a genuine part of the team from day 1;
  • Help the new employee be successful in his job as soon as possible;
  • Increase the retention rate of the company;
  • Increase the general wellbeing of employees;

Even though remote onboarding takes place virtually, the process has the same objectives as face-to-face onboarding. Since remote working employees don't have as many opportunities to organically integrate into the company processes and culture, good onboarding is even more essential. But unfortunately still overlooked by so many HR-managers and business owners.
